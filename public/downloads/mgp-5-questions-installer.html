5 Questions Smart Homeowners Ask
Before Hiring a Concrete Coating Installer

The questions a real pro will welcome — and a part-timer will dodge.
MGP Coatings · 94 Five-Star Google Reviews · CSLB #1061424 · San Luis Obispo County

You're about to invest meaningfully in your garage, pool deck, or driveway. The wrong installer can cost you twice — once when you pay, and again when you're scraping a peeling coating off your concrete in 2027.

These are the five questions we recommend every San Luis Obispo homeowner ask before signing anything. The right installer welcomes all of them. If your installer dodges any of these, find another bid.

1What chemistry are you using — epoxy or polyurea?

This is the single most important question, and most homeowners don't even know to ask it.

  • Epoxy is what 80% of installers use because it's cheap. It also yellows in UV, gets brittle in cold, and typically fails in 3–5 years on a daily-driven garage or sunny pool deck.
  • Polyurea is roughly 4× tougher than epoxy, stays flexible across temperature swings, doesn't yellow under UV, and lasts 15+ years when paired with a polyaspartic topcoat.

Most "1-day garage floor" ads you see are epoxy with a polyaspartic top — looks the same when wet, fails very differently after three winters.

Right answer: A clean, confident response naming both layers. "We use a polyurea base with a polyaspartic topcoat" is what you want to hear.
Red flag: "It's all the same chemistry" or "Epoxy is fine for residential." Both are wrong. Walk away.

2How long does the install actually take, start to finish?

The "1-day garage floor" claim is everywhere. The truth is in the prep.

  • The honest answer: 1–2 working days, including diamond-grinding the existing surface, repairing cracks and pitting, applying the basecoat with flake broadcast, and the topcoat — with proper cure windows in between.
  • Walk-on: typically 24 hours after the topcoat. Drive-on: 3 days. Fully cured: 5–7 days.
Right answer: A clear timeline that includes mechanical surface prep (grinding, not just acid-etching), repair of any cracks, and a defined cure schedule.
Red flag: "We can knock it out in 4 hours." That means no real prep — and prep is 80% of why a coating actually lasts.

3Are you licensed and insured in California — and what's your license number?

This sounds basic. It isn't.

  • California requires a CSLB (Contractors State License Board) license for any concrete coating job over $500.
  • An unlicensed installer leaves you legally exposed: damage to your property, injury on your driveway, vanishing with your deposit — you have no recourse.
  • Verify the license yourself at cslb.ca.gov before any deposit is paid. Confirm it's active, in their name, and has no recent disciplinary actions.
Right answer: A license number stated upfront, on their website, on the contract — and an active liability insurance certificate they'll happily email you. (MGP Coatings: CSLB #1061424.)
Red flag: "I'm working under a buddy's license" or any reluctance to put a license number in writing. Both mean you have zero protection.

4What does your warranty actually cover, and is it transferable?

Warranty language is where lazy installers hide.

  • Manufacturer-only warranties (typically "1 year on materials, labor not covered") are common — and effectively useless. The materials cost is roughly 5% of your project. If a coating fails, the labor to redo it is what hurts.
  • Installer-backed warranties are what matter. Ask specifically: What's covered? Peeling? Delamination? Hot-tire pickup? UV yellowing? For how many years? And — critically — is it transferable if you sell your home?
Right answer: A written warranty (not verbal), on company letterhead, with specifics on what's covered and for how long. An installer who's been in business long enough to honor it. Clarity on transferability.
Red flag: "We just stand behind our work" with nothing in writing, or a "lifetime" warranty from a brand-new business that may not exist in 5 years. Verbal promises don't survive a flooded garage.

5Can I see 50+ verified local reviews from the last 12 months?

Anyone can show you 5 cherry-picked testimonials. Smart homeowners look for depth — real volume across recent jobs in your area.

  • What to ask for: A direct link to their Google Business Profile, not screenshots. You want to see the full pattern — number of reviews, recency, response rate, and a few low-star reviews to see how they handle complaints.
  • Local matters: Reviews from out-of-county jobs tell you nothing about whether they'll pull permits properly, work with your HOA, or be available for a 2-year warranty call.
Right answer: 50+ Google reviews, the majority 5-star, recent (within the last 1–2 years), and from your county or neighboring counties. (MGP Coatings: 94 verified five-star Google reviews across San Luis Obispo County.)
Red flag: Only screenshots, no live link. Or hundreds of reviews from a different state — that's a franchise that swapped owners, not a local team you can call.
The bottom line: If an installer can't or won't answer all five of these clearly and in writing, walk away. The right installer welcomes every one of these questions — because the answers are how they earn your trust.
