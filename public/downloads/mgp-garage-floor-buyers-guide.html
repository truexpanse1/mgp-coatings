Part 1 — The 5 questions every coating contractor hopes you won't ask

1. "Walk me through your prep process in detail."

The answer you want: Diamond grinding to bare concrete, followed by a calcium chloride moisture test, crack repair with structural epoxy, and a full vacuum-down before primer.

Red flags: "Acid etching," "we just power-wash it," or vagueness. 85% of coating failures trace back to prep that got skipped.

2. "Can I see the data sheet for the top coat you'll use?"

The answer you want: A named product — not a generic. For Central Coast garages, you want polyaspartic (not straight epoxy) as the top coat for UV stability and hot tire resistance.

Red flags: "It's just a standard 2-part epoxy." Straight epoxy yellows and chalks in UV. It also fails from hot tire pickup within 18-36 months.

3. "What's the warranty, and is it written?"

The answer you want: A written, named warranty on both material AND workmanship, honored by the contractor who installed it (not a manufacturer in another state).

Red flags: "Oh, it'll last forever," or "the manufacturer warrants it." If it's not on paper, it doesn't exist.

4. "How long have you personally been installing, and who will actually be on my job?"

The answer you want: A contractor with 5+ years of direct installation experience who will be on your job, not sending a crew of subcontractors.

Red flags: A salesperson who can't answer either question, or "we use a crew network."

5. "Can I see three recent local installs and talk to those homeowners?"

The answer you want: Real addresses, real homeowners willing to talk.

Red flags: National before/after stock photos, or unwillingness to connect you with anyone.

Part 2 — Why polyaspartic beats epoxy for Central Coast garages

Epoxy and polyaspartic both have their place. But for a Central Coast garage — which is exposed to UV bleed-through from open garage doors, summer heat cycles, hot tires parking directly on the coating, and salt air if you're near the coast — straight epoxy fails. It yellows. It chalks. It picks up with hot tires. Polyaspartic was engineered specifically for these failure modes.

The professional answer: Use 100% solids epoxy as the basecoat (it fills and bonds to concrete better than anything else), then top it with polyaspartic (UV-stable, hot-tire-resistant, cures in hours). You get the best of both systems.

That's what MGP Coatings installs. It's not the cheapest option. It's the only one with a 15-year warranty in Central Coast conditions.

Part 4 — The DIY kit trap

Home Depot and Lowe's sell "garage floor coating kits" for $150–$300. It feels like a win: save $3,000+, do it yourself over a weekend. Here's what actually happens in 90%+ of DIY installs:

  1. Prep is skipped or minimized. Acid etching doesn't open concrete pores the way diamond grinding does. Adhesion is marginal from day one.
  2. The product is thin and consumer-grade. Kit coatings are typically 1-coat, water-based acrylic or low-solids epoxy. Real garage systems are 15-25 mils thick. Kits are 3-5 mils.
  3. UV and hot tire fail you fast. Within one summer, you'll see yellowing. Within 18 months, hot tire pickup. Within 3 years, the coating is peeling in patches.
  4. Removal costs more than a pro install. Now you need a contractor to strip the failed coating before installing a real one. That removal can run $1,000–$2,500 on its own.

Math: $300 now = $4,000–$6,000 later. The DIY kit is almost always the most expensive option over the life of the floor.

Part 5 — The apples-to-apples comparison checklist

When you get three quotes, line them up against these 10 criteria:

  • Is the prep method diamond grinding (not acid etch, not scrubbing)?
  • Is moisture testing included before coating?
  • Is crack repair included (structural epoxy fill)?
  • Is the top coat polyaspartic (not acrylic, not straight epoxy)?
  • Is the full system thickness 20+ mils?
  • Is the warranty written, named, and cover both material and workmanship?
  • Is the warranty length 10+ years (15+ is the standard for a full pro system)?
  • Will the contractor who bid also install the job?
  • Are local references available (not just online stock photos)?
  • Is the contractor licensed and insured (CSLB license lookup: cslb.ca.gov)?

A contractor who checks all 10 is worth paying for. A contractor who checks 4 or fewer is about to cost you more than they save you.
