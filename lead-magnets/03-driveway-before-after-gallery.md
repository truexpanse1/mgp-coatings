## Project 01 — Templeton · 950 sqft · $4,800

**Before:** 15-year-old broom-finish concrete with oil staining in three spots, two hairline cracks running the length of the driveway, and heavy efflorescence near the garage door.

**What we did:** Diamond grind, structural epoxy crack repair, one-coat 100% solids primer, broadcast chip (Silverstone blend), polyaspartic topcoat.

**Timeline:** Day 1 prep and primer. Day 2 chip broadcast and topcoat. Drivable in 72 hours.

**Matt's notes:** *"Classic Templeton driveway. The efflorescence told us the concrete was wicking moisture — we did an extended moisture test to make sure we weren't locking water in under the coating. That's why this job took a full second day. Homeowner texted me six months later to say the coating makes the whole house look 10 years newer."*

## Project 02 — Paso Robles · 1,200 sqft · $6,400

**Before:** Exposed aggregate driveway with heavy fading from UV and minor surface spalling near the sidewalk.

**What we did:** Pressure wash, surface stabilization primer, color-tinted polyaspartic (tan with dark flake), satin finish.

**Timeline:** 1 day.

**Matt's notes:** *"Exposed aggregate is tricky — you can't grind it flat without destroying the texture the homeowner paid for 20 years ago. We used a penetrating primer that locks the existing surface and gives the polyaspartic something to grab. Looks like a premium textured finish now instead of faded rock."*

## Project 03 — San Luis Obispo · 780 sqft · $4,200

**Before:** Someone had tried to "seal" it with a clear acrylic 4 years ago. It had peeled in patches exposing contaminated concrete beneath.

**What we did:** Full chemical strip of old sealer, diamond grind, spot moisture test, full system install with polyaspartic topcoat.

**Timeline:** 2 days (strip took most of day 1).

**Matt's notes:** *"This is the kind of job that's why I say 'cheap now = expensive later.' The acrylic sealer job was $800 four years ago. The strip-and-redo cost this homeowner $1,400 more than it would have if they'd just done it right the first time. Painful to watch."*

## Project 04 — Atascadero · 1,450 sqft · $7,900

**Before:** Cracked concrete with one settlement issue near the street, heavy tire marks, and a homeowner who wanted it to look like the garage coating we'd done inside.

**What we did:** Full crack chase and epoxy repair, color-matched polyaspartic to the interior garage (dark gray with copper flake), satin finish, sealed to meet the existing garage coating at the threshold.

**Timeline:** 2 days.

**Matt's notes:** *"Matching an exterior driveway to an interior garage floor is a signature move. We ran the same chip blend across the threshold so the two spaces read as one. The homeowner said it's the first thing people comment on when they pull in."*

## Which finish adds the most curb appeal?

Of the 12 projects here, the ones homeowners reported the strongest reactions to were the **dark gray polyaspartic with metallic or flake broadcast**. It reads as modern, high-contrast against most home exteriors, and hides future dirt/tire marks better than lighter colors.

Second place: **warm tan with earth-tone chip** — especially on Spanish-style or Mediterranean homes where it complements the existing palette.

Third place: **high-gloss clear over exposed aggregate** — this preserves a texture many homeowners already like while dramatically deepening color saturation.

## One thing Matt would do differently

**On about 2 out of 12 jobs, Matt wishes he'd pushed harder on extending the coating under the garage door threshold.**

*"When we coat right up to the garage door but stop at the threshold, you get a visible line where the old concrete meets the new coating. It's small, but it bugs perfectionists. On jobs where the homeowner is also doing the garage floor, we now run the coating across the threshold as one continuous surface. Costs marginally more. Worth every penny."*

Practical lesson for you: **if you're considering both a garage floor AND a driveway coating, do them together.** The continuous install is cleaner and the combined quote is almost always better than two separate ones.
